## The Competitive Landscape

### What's out there

**Tool courses** (Designlab, Miami Ad School, Academy of Art University, ELVTR)
Teaching specific AI tools — Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, Firefly, node-based workflows. These are the Adobe-equivalent courses: here's the software, here's how to use it. Output-focused. Tool-dependent. Will become obsolete as tools change.

**Prompting courses** (Udemy, Coursera/IBM)
Longer prompts, better outputs. Prompt engineering basics, Chain-of-Thought, few-shot techniques. These are teaching the symptom, not the cause. The problem starts before the prompt.

**Institutional programs** (Parsons, UCLA Extension, Cambridge PACE, NC State)
Academic exploration of authorship, ethics, creative agency in the AI era. Thoughtful. Slow. 2-3 years from having a product.

### Closest competitor

**ELVTR — AI-Aided Graphic Design** (May 14–June 23, 2026)
Instructor: Evan Boehm, Group Creative Director at BUCK.
Teaching "strategic scaffolds," "world logic," "evaluative systems," creative control and authorship. Same territory, different angle. Still tool and workflow focused. Still art director register. Likely $1,200+. Worth watching.

This is the one course in the market that's moving toward the same problem. It's not there yet.

### What nobody has

- Research foundation with DOIs (six published papers, peer-reviewed cognitive science)
- Accommodation Design derived from special education pedagogy — a provable origin, not a concept
- The persona stack / values declaration frame ("designed to BE" vs "designed FOR")
- Applied practice on the student's own real project — not fictional examples, not a portfolio
- The full six-domain ladder with Prosthetic Cognition as the declared destination
- A methodology that works in any chat interface — no terminal required
- Victore as a potential distribution channel into the artist/illustrator audience

## The Window

**Estimated runway:** 18-24 months before a competent independent practitioner is in this lane.

The bootcamps won't get here — they're too tool-dependent and their audience expects tool instruction. The institutions are too slow. The real threat is one sharp practitioner who has the same insight and moves fast.

**The window closes when:**
- Someone publishes research-backed methodology for creative practitioners with the same depth
- A well-known creative educator (Boehm, someone from SVA, a major YouTuber) synthesizes the same insight without the research foundation
- The AI labs build enough methodology scaffolding into their interfaces that the problem partially solves itself

**The window stays open while:**
- The research moat compounds (citations, DOIs, peer acknowledgment)
- The founding cohort produces real results that can be named and shown
- The Victore relationship (or similar) produces an organic case study
- The methodology expands to serve practitioners the tool courses ignore (novelists, illustrators, musicians, educators)

---

## Moat-Building Priorities

**In order of impact:**

1. **Founding cohort results.** 50 students doing real work on real projects. Their before/after is the artifact. One Rachel with a published chapter, one Maya with a brand system, one David with a team protocol — that's worth more than any marketing funnel.

2. **Victore case study.** If the relationship develops into a session, and he produces something, and he talks about how he got there — that's organic credibility into an audience no tool course can reach. Don't rush it. Let it develop from the work.

3. **DOI citations.** The papers are published. As the field matures, citations compound. Being the first published framework in this specific territory is a durable advantage.

4. **Category ownership.** "Creative infrastructure for AI-mediated work" is an unclaimed category. Joinery should own the phrase before someone else names it. The Engineering Intent series, the research papers, the site copy — all of it should reinforce the category name.

5. **Curriculum depth.** The six-domain ladder is defensible in a way a single course isn't. Each course teaches something the others don't. The Workbench extends into territory (MCP, API integrations, SVG pipelines, structured output) that tool courses will never teach because they don't understand the methodology layer beneath it.

## Pricing Context

The ELVTR course (closest competitor) is likely $1,200+. Parsons certificate programs run $3,000-5,000. Udemy prompting courses are $15-30.

Joinery's founding cohort at $299 is deliberately below market to build the first cohort of results. The price should increase after founding cohort closes. The research foundation and personal review from the founder justify a significantly higher price than the prompting courses. The ceiling is the institutional programs — Joinery offers more applied practice than Parsons at a fraction of the cost.

## What This Means for Decisions

**On timing:** Move now. The founding cohort in May is the right call. Don't wait for the curriculum to be complete — launch with Foundations and build the ladder as students move through it.

**On partnerships:** Victore is the right first partnership precisely because he's not in the tool-course world. His credibility with artists and illustrators opens a distribution channel that no competitor has. Approach as a peer, not a pitch.

**On pricing:** Raise after founding cohort. The $299 founding price is a relationship investment, not a market rate. Post-cohort pricing should reflect the research foundation and the results.

**On scope:** Don't chase the design market exclusively. The methodology works for anyone whose primary output is original thought. Writers, musicians, illustrators, educators — these are underserved by every existing course. Victore's audience is the proof of concept.

**On the Engineering Intent series:** This is a publishing/content play that establishes category ownership beyond the course. Prioritize after founding cohort is underway.
