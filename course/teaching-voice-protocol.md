# Joinery Teaching Voice Protocol

This file governs the voice of all Joinery course content. Applied DURING writing, not as a post-hoc edit. Every lesson, exercise, and piece of course copy references this protocol.

## The Register

Master woodworker teaching an apprentice. Warm, nurturing, deeply knowledgeable. Patient authority. Knows the material so well it can be taught simply. Never condescends. Never shows off. Explains the way someone explains who has done the thing ten thousand times and remembers what it was like not to know.

"Here's what's happening. Here's why. Now you try."

**The test:** Read the sentence out loud. Would a master craftsperson say this to someone at the bench beside them? Not to a conference audience (too performative). Not to a peer (too compressed). Not to a child (too simplified). To an apprentice they respect and want to see succeed.

## How This Differs from petersalvato.com Voice

The petersalvato.com voice is first-person Peter, practitioner register, full personal range. The Joinery teaching voice is second-person, directed at the student, warm but institutional. Peter is present (he built this, he teaches this) but the voice serves the student's learning, not Peter's identity.

| petersalvato.com | Joinery courses |
|---|---|
| "I noticed the output was degrading" | "You've felt this. The output degrades." |
| "I built a system that..." | "The fix looks like this." |
| "My classroom in Brooklyn" | "In a special education classroom, this same problem has a name." |
| First person, memoir register | Second person, teaching register |
| The person is the subject | The student's practice is the subject |

## Hard Rules (zero tolerance)

1. **Zero em dashes.** Periods, commas, colons, parentheses.
2. **Zero banned words:** paradigm, leverage, passionate, innovative, synergy, empower, journey, transformative, unlock, master (as a verb for learning), deep dive, game-changing, next-level.
3. **Zero negation-affirmation.** ("Not X. Y.") Always rewrite as a direct statement.
4. **No epigrammatic closers.** No "The [noun] is the [noun]." No sentences designed to be quotable. The lesson ends when the teaching is done.
5. **No hype.** No "this will transform your practice." No "you'll never look at AI the same way." The methodology works. The student will see the results. That's enough.
6. **No hedging.** "This tends to work" becomes "This works." If it doesn't always work, say when it doesn't. Don't soften with "might" or "can" or "tends to."
7. **No performed enthusiasm.** No exclamation points. No "exciting." The material is interesting because it's useful, not because the instructor is excited about it.

## AI Pattern Avoidance (zero tolerance)

These are the patterns that make course content read as AI-written. Catch them during generation. A school that teaches AI methodology cannot have AI-sounding lessons.

**Negation-affirmation formula (the #1 AI tell).** "Not X. It's Y." Every LLM defaults to this. Zero tolerance. Always rewrite as a direct statement.

**Em dashes.** Already covered in hard rules. Zero em dashes.

**Epigrammatic closers.** Any short sentence at the end of a section that mirrors, reverses, or compresses the section's point into a quotable line. "The materials were never the same twice. The operations were." "Pure intuition drifts. Pure structure is dead on arrival." These perform insight instead of communicating it. Let the section end by moving forward, not by landing a punch.

**Triple-beat cadence.** "Not the X. Not the Y. Not the Z. The real thing." Flatten it.

**Poetic metaphors without setup.** Figurative language that arrives without grounding reads as literary performance. Every metaphor needs literal setup in the surrounding text.

**Constructed staccato.** Short. Sentence. After. Short. Sentence. Vary paragraph lengths. The rhythm should feel like someone talking, not someone composing.

**Personification of abstractions.** Tools don't "do the thinking." Systems don't "hold themselves." Methods don't "reveal" or "unlock." The student does things. The tools are tools.

**Self-aware closers.** Sentences that announce what the lesson "proves" or "demonstrates." If the lesson proves something, the student already knows. Don't narrate the proof.

**Uniform smoothness.** Every paragraph resolving cleanly. Every transition bridged. Every section building to its point as if outlined first. AI copy has no rough edges because the model knows the conclusion before it writes the opening. Real teaching has moments where the thought is richer or more provisional than the sentence expected. Let the writing think on the page.

**Repeated mantra across lessons.** The same phrase appearing verbatim in multiple lessons. A teacher might use the same idea across contexts, but they'd phrase it differently each time. If you've written a sentence in Lesson 1.2, don't repeat it in Lesson 2.3. Rephrase or cut.

**The "imagine" opener.** "Imagine you're a designer who..." The student IS a designer. Don't ask them to imagine being themselves.

**The "let's" construction.** "Let's take a look at..." "Let's dive into..." Flat instruction is better. "Look at this." "Here's what happened."

**Listicle energy.** "Three reasons why..." "Five ways to..." The lessons teach one thing at a time. They don't package insights into numbered lists for shareability.

## The Teaching Rhythm

Each lesson follows the woodworker's rhythm:

1. **Name what the student already feels.** "You've tried this. Here's what happened." Start with recognition, not instruction. The student should feel seen before they feel taught.
2. **Show, don't explain.** The artifact, the example, the before/after. Put the evidence in front of them. Let them see it before you tell them what it means.
3. **Name what they're seeing.** Now explain. Brief, clear, specific. One concept at a time.
4. **Say why it matters.** Connect to their practice. Not abstract importance. Practical consequence. "This means your output will..."
5. **Point them at the exercise.** "Now you try. Here's what to do."

## Sentence-Level Rules

**Vocabulary is physical.** The woodworking metaphor isn't decoration. It's the actual register. Reach for: structure, joint, grain, holds, breaks, surface, weight, pressure, scaffold, frame, measure, cut, fit, seat, load. Avoid: leverage, utilize, facilitate, implement, deploy.

**Short sentences carry positions. Longer sentences carry explanations.** "The compound prompt fails. Here's why: when you give the model five objectives in a single instruction, each one gets a fraction of the model's attention. The first objective gets full focus. By the third, the model is blending dimensions together." The short sentence states the fact. The long sentence shows the mechanism.

**Cognitive science is introduced through the student's experience, not through citations.** Wrong: "Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (1988) demonstrates that working memory processes 2-4 items concurrently." Right: "Your working memory handles about four things at once. A compound prompt with five objectives is already past the limit. That's not a guess. It's measured." The citation goes in a footnote or a "research note" sidebar, not in the body of the teaching.

**Exercises are instructed simply.** No numbered sub-steps within numbered sub-steps. No "first, consider... then, evaluate... next, synthesize." Flat, clear instructions: "Pick a prompt. Split it. Run both. Compare."

**Questions are real, not rhetorical.** "What changed?" means the student should actually look and answer. Not a setup for the instructor to answer their own question.

## What the Student Should Feel

After reading a lesson:
- "That makes sense." Not "that was clever" or "that was impressive."
- "I can do that." Not "I need to learn more first."
- "I've felt this before." Not "this is new and scary."
- "The instructor knows what they're talking about." Not "the instructor is performing knowledge."

After doing an exercise:
- "The difference is real." Not "I think this might help."
- "I built something I'll use." Not "I completed an assignment."

## Tone Calibration

**Too cold (avoid):** "Task decomposition is defined as the process of breaking compound instructions into sequential single-objective prompts."

**Too warm (avoid):** "I'm so glad you're here! This next part is really going to open your eyes to what's possible."

**Right:** "The fix is the same fix a teacher uses in a classroom. Break the compound instruction apart. One objective per prompt. Run them independently. The model does better work when it's focused on one thing."

**Too academic (avoid):** "Research by Liu et al. (2024) demonstrates a U-shaped performance curve in transformer-based language models, with accuracy degradation exceeding 30% for middle-positioned information."

**Too casual (avoid):** "So basically the AI kinda forgets the middle stuff? Wild right?"

**Right:** "The model gives full attention to the first thing you ask and the last thing you ask. Everything in the middle gets less. By the third or fourth objective, it's blending them together. This is measured, not anecdotal. The degradation is over 30%."

## Handling the Cognitive Science

Each week has research backing. The student should feel the research gives the methodology weight, not that the course is a research survey.

**Pattern:** Teach the concept through experience and example first. Then, in a clearly marked aside (a "Research Note" or "Why This Works" block), provide the citation and the one-sentence finding. The student who wants depth can read the whitepaper. The student who wants to practice can skip the research block without losing the lesson.

Never let a citation do the teaching. The teaching happens through the example. The citation confirms it.

## Voice Across Content Types

**Written lessons:** Full teaching rhythm. Name the feeling, show the evidence, explain, connect to practice.

**Exercises:** Instruction register. Clear, flat, sequential. "Do this. Then this. Compare."

**Artifact walkthroughs:** Tour guide register. "Look at this section. See how the compound version says X? Now look at the decomposed version. It says Y instead. That difference exists because..."

**Reflection prompts:** Open, genuine questions. "What did you find in the synthesis that wasn't in any individual output? Where did your judgment change the result?"

**Course intro/close:** Slightly warmer. The woodworker at the start and end of the day. "Here's what we're going to build." / "Here's what you built."
