# Roughing It Hot Sauce — Creative Direction Brief

Compiled from tracked decisions made during specialist-output review. Each decision below was made by stepping through one specialist's findings at a time, reacting, and recording the call.

---

## Color

The specialist built the palette as a two-color print job (Harvest Soil + Cream) matching small-batch flexo production realities. That economic logic holds. But the core palette missed the brand's heart: the chiles themselves.

Green is the primary brand color. The chile plant, the farm, the growth. Red is the accent. The heat, the fruit. Harvest Soil and Cream stay as the baseline substrate. Off-black for all text, not pure black (reads warmer on cream and kraft).

The seasonal per-sauce accents (Pepper Green, Cayenne, Ember, Aged Obsidian) layer on top of this structure. The core palette now carries the chile's lifecycle: green plant, red fruit, on earth-toned ground.

**The specialist's "no heat-signaling reds" recommendation is overridden.** Red is present, but it signals the fruit, not danger. The distinction matters. This is not a cartoon flame. It is the color of a cayenne at harvest.

## Typography

Pairing 2: Freight Text Pro + IBM Plex Sans.

Freight Text carries warmer editorial weight than Canela (Pairing 1) and offers wider weight support (Book, Medium, Semibold, Bold) for label hierarchy. IBM Plex Sans is a humanist grotesk with enough warmth to pair with Freight without creating a cold/warm clash.

The specialist's monospace recommendation (Söhne Mono for field-notebook signal) is not adopted for the primary pairing. IBM Plex Sans handles data and metadata. If the field-notebook signal is needed on specific surfaces (harvest date stamps, batch logs), monospace can be introduced as a tertiary face, not as the secondary.

Pairing evaluated using free-font approximations (Lora + IBM Plex Sans) in a type-comparison playground across four substrates (cream, kraft, white, dark). Pairing dynamics confirmed visually before committing. Actual fonts to be licensed when production begins.

## Logo

**The specialist's "no icon" recommendation is overridden.** The reasoning was sound for a generic hot sauce brand (pepper imagery is a crutch in the category). Roughing It is different. Tess grows the chiles herself. The chile is not a decorative motif. It is evidence of what the farm produces.

Two directions to explore on paper:

1. Chile silhouette integrated as a letterform within "Roughing It" (replacing or modifying a letter).
2. Chile cross-section as an external round mark, functioning as a stamp or seal.

Both explored pen and paper first. The hand-drawn exploration is the process. Final mark comes from the strongest sketch, digitized with minimal cleanup. The specialist's constraints hold: reproducible on a napkin, works at bottle-cap size and market-stall scale, one color on any surface.

## Imagery

The specialist's three-category structure is accepted as a working draft:

- 40% process photography (hands, tools, fermentation, harvest)
- 35% seasonal farm photography (same plots across 4 seasons, same angles when possible)
- 25% in-context product photography (bottle in use in a real kitchen, not on white seamless)

Natural light only. No studio setups. Image library refreshes quarterly with the sauce release. All hand-lettered material scanned at 600 DPI grayscale, no auto-leveling.

Ratios are provisional. They may need rebalancing once real photography is in place and we see how the brand feels at scale. The structure holds; the percentages flex.

## Layout

Classic and dense. The label is the primary surface. Everything else descends from its logic.

The aesthetic ancestor is Henderson's Sign Painter. Vintage signpainter craft and composition inform the visual language: hand-lettered hierarchy, dense information layouts, type that earns its space. This is not faux-vintage distressing (which the specialist correctly banned). The reference is about how signpainters organized information on a surface with confidence and density, not about artificially aging the design.

The system stays fixed across seasons. Only the accent color bar and the photography rotate. The stability of the system is the identity. The seasonal changes read because everything else holds still.

---

## Governing Principles (cross-specialist)

**1. The chile lifecycle governs the palette.** Green (growth, plant, farm) and red (fruit, heat, harvest) are structurally present, not just accent colors. The brand's visual story follows the pepper from plant to bottle.

**2. Density is the signal.** Dense label, dense type, dense information. The brand has more to say than it has room for. Whitespace is earned, not default. This connects the signpainter aesthetic (Henderson), the label-first layout system, and the farming-provenance positioning.

**3. Evidence over decoration.** The chile mark is evidence (Tess grows these). The process photography is evidence (this is how the sauce is made). The monospace data is evidence (these are the actual batch numbers and harvest dates). Every visual element answers a factual question rather than creating a mood.
