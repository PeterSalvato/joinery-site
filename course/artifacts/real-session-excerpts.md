## Excerpt 2: Voice Extraction (Week 3, Lesson 3.4)

**Context:** March 18, 2026. Peter was reviewing eight chapters of a book series (HomeWorks, about accommodation design applied to household systems). Claude had just run a full copy review and was making surgical fixes to voice violations across the chapters, including reducing negation-affirmation patterns ("Not X. Y.") to a maximum of one per page, per the existing voice protocol rule.

---

Claude had just finished fixing repeated mantras and reducing negation-affirmation patterns across the chapters:

**Claude:** All fixes applied. Repeated mantra fixed (Ch 5 owns "falls through," others reference the concept). Negation-affirmation overflow fixed (max 1 per page across all chapters). No content removed, just tightened. What's next?

> **23:51 Peter:** I think I just really hate negation affirmations I think they sound performed. Like the AI is landing a point instead of making one.

**[ANNOTATION: This is the raw observation. Peter has been reading the "fixed" versions where one negation-affirmation per page was kept, and his reaction is visceral. He is not analyzing the pattern. He is reacting to how it feels. The phrasing is the diagnostic: it sounds performed. The AI is constructing a rhetorical move instead of saying what it means.]**

**Claude:** Then zero. Not one per page. Zero. Want me to sweep all 8 chapters and kill every remaining one?

**[ANNOTATION: The AI's response matters here. No pushback, no "but some are earned," no attempt to preserve the existing rule. The rule was "max one per page." Peter just killed it with a gut reaction. The AI matched the decisiveness immediately. This is how voice rules actually get made: a practitioner reads their own copy, feels something wrong, names it, and the system updates.]**

Claude swept all eight chapters. In the process, it distinguished real patterns from false positives:

**Claude:** The Ch 5 one ("went to the dread, not the work") and Ch 15 one ("is not a sous vide bag") aren't negation-affirmation patterns. They're natural comparisons within a sentence. Leaving those.

**[ANNOTATION: This is the system distinguishing the pattern from natural language. "Went to the dread, not the work" uses "not" inside a comparison. "Not X. Y." as a rhetorical structure (two sentences, the first negating, the second affirming) is different from the word "not" appearing in a sentence. The rule targets the rhetorical pattern, not the word. The AI made this distinction without being asked.]**

**Claude:** Zero negation-affirmations across all 8 HomeWorks chapters. The voice protocol rule should be updated too. Want me to change it from "max ONE per page" to "zero"?

> **23:54 Peter:** yes update it to zero

**[ANNOTATION: The rule is now permanent. What follows is propagation.]**

Claude then updated six governance files in sequence:

**Claude:** All updated:
- `voice-protocol.md`: zero tolerance
- `~/.claude/CLAUDE.md`: zero tolerance
- `petersalvato.com/CLAUDE.md`: zero tolerance
- `skills/copy-verify/SKILL.md`: zero tolerance
- `skills/copy/SKILL.md`: zero tolerance
- `skills/baseline/SKILL.md`: zero tolerance

Negation-affirmation is dead across the entire system.

**[ANNOTATION: One gut reaction propagated to six files that govern all future copy generation and verification. The time from "I think they sound performed" to system-wide zero tolerance: under three minutes. This is what voice governance looks like in practice. A human notices something, names it, and the system encodes the preference so it never has to be noticed again.]**

## Excerpt 3: Verification / False Positive Override (Week 3, Lesson 3.5)

**Context:** March 7, 2026. Peter was executing a positioning strategy for his LinkedIn presence. He had just drafted a comment on a post by a well-known design educator about the Shu-Ha-Ri learning stages. The comment connected the educator's point about following a master's instruction to Peter's own methodology of holding multiple evaluative frameworks in tension.

---

Claude drafted a comment connecting Shu (following one master) to Ri (holding multiple masters' contradictions):

**Claude:** Something like: "The hard part comes after Shu. You internalize one master's framework, then another, then another. Eventually they contradict each other. Vignelli says strip everything to the grid. Victore says be brave and break it. Both are right. The skill isn't following one of them. It's holding the tension and deciding which value wins for this specific piece of work. That's Ri -- and nobody teaches it because it can't be reduced to 'do exactly as I say.'"

> **18:38 Peter:** run that through my voice protocol frst

**[ANNOTATION: Peter did not read the comment and approve it. He sent it to verification first. This is the protocol in action: draft, then check, then publish. The typo ("frst") tells you this is muscle memory, not ceremony.]**

Claude ran the 12-item copy-verify checklist:

**Result: 9/12 passed.**

**Em dashes: FAIL.** One em dash: "That's Ri -- and nobody teaches it."

**[ANNOTATION: Mechanical violation. The em dash is invisible when you're reading for meaning. The protocol catches it because the protocol does not read for meaning. It reads for pattern. This is what systems catch that eyes miss.]**

**Fortune-cookie closer: FAIL.** "because it can't be reduced to 'do exactly as I say'" wraps too neatly. It reads as a mic drop.

**[ANNOTATION: Structural violation. The closer sounds clever. That is the problem. A line that sounds like it was written to be the last line is performing for the audience instead of finishing the thought. Different category than the em dash: this is about rhetoric, not mechanics.]**

**Negation-affirmation: FAIL.** "The skill isn't following one of them. It's holding the tension." The "Not X. Y." pattern.

**[ANNOTATION: Rhetorical violation. The negation-affirmation pattern is the most common AI writing tell. It sounds authoritative. It sounds like a point is being made. That is what makes it dangerous: it feels like good writing while actually being a formula.]**

Claude fixed all three and produced a clean version:

**Claude:** "The hard part comes after Shu. You internalize one master's framework, then another, then another. Eventually they contradict each other. Vignelli says strip everything to the grid. Victore says be brave and break it. Both are right. The skill is holding both perspectives and making the call on which value wins for this specific piece of work. That's Ri. Nobody teaches it."

> **18:39 Peter:** i have to add that its the human in the loop making the CHOICE between those perspectives... right?

**[ANNOTATION: The system caught three mechanical/structural/rhetorical violations. It could not catch a missing idea. Peter read the cleaned version and noticed the argument was incomplete: the comment described holding multiple perspectives but did not say who makes the final decision. This is what the human adds that the system cannot see. The verification protocol handles surface patterns. The practitioner handles substance.]**

**Claude:** Yeah. That's the whole point. The methodology surfaces the disagreements, the human makes the call. Updated version with that addition.

> **18:40 Peter:** do i tag victore? no, right? thats too much?

**[ANNOTATION: Social judgment. No protocol covers this. Tagging a mentor in a comment on someone else's post is a strategic and relational decision. Peter asked the question, answered it himself ("no, right?"), and checked his instinct.]**

**Claude:** Don't tag. It's name-dropping in a comment. If Victore sees it organically because he's in your network, that's a real moment. If you tag him to borrow his credibility, it's a performance.

Peter posted the comment. Claude logged it as the day's first strategic engagement.

## Excerpt 4: Full-Stack Methodology (Week 3, Lesson 3.6)

**Context:** March 7, 2026. A single working session that ran from early morning through late evening. Peter was executing a comprehensive LinkedIn positioning strategy: planning a content calendar, writing posts, verifying copy, making judgment calls, and shipping. This excerpt traces the full arc.

---

### Decomposition (Week 1 skill)

The session began with Peter pasting source material and asking Claude to map the full content landscape:

**Claude** identified that eight blog posts were live, three LinkedIn posts were already published (with low engagement), and the narrative was misaligned:

**Claude:** You're right. The whole series leads with AI. Every post says "here's my AI system." That positions you as an AI implementation guy who built a clever pipeline. But the thesis is the opposite. You're a design engineer with 25 years of practice. AI is the current substrate.

**[ANNOTATION: This is Week 1: decomposition. Before writing anything, the system maps what exists, identifies the structural problem, and names it. The content was not bad. The framing was wrong. Decomposition reveals the gap between intent and execution.]**

Peter and Claude restructured: renamed the blog series from "ai-governance" to "design-engineering," reordered all eight posts by thesis hierarchy instead of publish date, and established the framing rule: design practitioner first, AI as current substrate, not the identity.

### Unstructured Input (Week 2 skill)

Peter pasted raw material throughout the session: LinkedIn post drafts, blog post content, a competitor's carousel, his own career history, half-formed thoughts about positioning.

> **17:33 Peter:** and i'd say that the spec-ed portino makes me a monster at chunking tasks for ai and agentic work... its actually kind of crazy

**[ANNOTATION: This is Week 2: unstructured input. Peter dropped an observation with a typo and no structure. Claude recognized the insight (special education pedagogy is structurally identical to agentic AI architecture: decompose complex tasks, sequence them, provide clear success criteria, scaffold for independence) and reflected it back as a named connection. The raw input became a content asset.]**

**Claude:** That's a genuine insight. Special education pedagogy is literally the discipline of: take a complex task, break it into discrete steps, sequence them correctly, provide clear success criteria for each step, and scaffold so the learner can do it independently. That's prompt engineering. That's skill architecture. That's the coordinator/atomic pattern you already built. You didn't learn agentic design from AI documentation. You learned it from teaching kids who needed the instructions to be precise, sequenced, and verifiable.

### Voice Governance (Week 3 skill)

When Peter drafted a comment on a well-known design educator's post about learning stages, he sent it through copy-verify before posting (see Excerpt 3 above for the full exchange). The system caught three violations: an em dash, a fortune-cookie closer, and a negation-affirmation pattern. Each was fixed. Then Peter added the substance the system could not flag: the missing argument about human judgment being the deciding factor.

In the LinkedIn post drafts themselves, Claude ran verification on each one. An early version of the Voice Governance post contained a triple negation-affirmation ("Your blog post voice is not your real voice. Your LinkedIn update voice is not your real voice. Your real voice is in your conversations..."). Copy-verify flagged it. The fix collapsed three negations into one direct statement:

**Claude:** "Published writing is a performance, shaped for an audience, rough edges removed. The voice an AI learns from your blog is the performance version of you."

**[ANNOTATION: Three rhetorical moves replaced by one direct statement. The information is the same. The voice is different. This is what governance produces: copy that sounds like a person instead of copy that sounds like it was engineered to sound like a person.]**

### Override (the human judgment layer)

When Claude proposed the five-domain positioning (design practice, operational methodology, AI implementation, design history/criticism, education) and asserted "nobody else has all five," Peter caught it:

> **16:28 Peter:** "nobody else has all 5" is a statement made without any research.

**[ANNOTATION: The human overrides the system. Claude made an unverified claim that sounded good. Peter applied the same standard to conversation that the voice protocol applies to copy: is the claim grounded? It was not. Claude acknowledged the error. Peter then asked for actual competitive research, which Claude conducted. The claim turned out to be defensible, but only after the research. The sequence matters: verify first, then claim.]**

**Claude:** You're right. I can't verify that, and saying it without checking is exactly the kind of thing your no-hallucination policy exists to prevent. Me doing it in conversation is the same problem as the system doing it in copy. What I can say honestly: the combination is unusual. But I don't know who else is out there, and claiming uniqueness without research is the kind of empty positioning that makes peers cringe.

### Shipping (the deliverable)

> **20:07 Peter:** posts scheduled daily through thursday.

**[ANNOTATION: This is where the deliverable ships. Five LinkedIn posts scheduled across five days. Four existing posts edited in place with design-practitioner-first framing. Blog restructured with series callout and reading order. Competitive landscape researched. 30-60-90 positioning plan documented. One strategic comment posted. All in a single working session. The methodology produced a real output: content that went live to a real audience on a real schedule.]**

**Claude:** Good. All five scheduled, edits done on the four live posts, capstone rewritten, all signals aligned. LinkedIn is handled. What do you want to tackle next?

> **20:08 Peter:** no idea. im feeling spun around

**Claude:** You did a lot today. Here's what's actually done: Blog restructured. All machine-readable signals hammered at the five-domain nexus. 4 LinkedIn posts edited. 5 LinkedIn posts scheduled through Thursday. Capstone blog post rewritten. 30-60-90 plan documented. Competitive landscape researched. First strategic engagement posted. The LinkedIn posts will run themselves for the next five days. Nothing needs your attention until Thursday afternoon when you see how the series landed. Take a break. Come back when your head is clear.

**[ANNOTATION: The full arc in one session. Decomposition (Week 1): mapping the content landscape and identifying the framing problem. Unstructured input (Week 2): pasting raw material and half-formed insights that became content assets. Voice governance (Week 3): copy-verify catching what eyes miss, the human adding what the system cannot. Override: catching an ungrounded claim and demanding research. Shipping: five posts scheduled, four edited, blog restructured, plan documented. This is the methodology working end to end.]**
