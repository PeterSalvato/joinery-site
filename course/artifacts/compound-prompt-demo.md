# Course Artifact: Compound vs Decomposed Prompt Demo

## The Setup

**Brand:** Mara Voss, freelance brand designer and letterer. Brooklyn-based. Launching her own studio called "Voss Type." Clients: independent food brands, breweries, record labels. Style: bold, hand-drawn typography with a confrontational edge.

**Note:** Mara Voss is a fictional persona created for this course exercise.

**The lesson:** A compound prompt makes the AI the creative director. Decomposed prompts make you the creative director with specialists reporting to you.

---

## Part 1: The Compound Prompt

> Act as a creative director. I'm Mara Voss, a freelance brand designer and letterer based in Brooklyn. I'm launching my own studio called Voss Type. My clients are independent food brands, breweries, and record labels. My style is bold, hand-drawn typography with a confrontational edge. Generate a visual branding guide for Voss Type that includes: recommended color palette with hex codes and psychological rationale, typography pairings with usage contexts, logo style direction, imagery guidelines, and layout principles for my website and social media. Make sure it reflects my hand-crafted aesthetic and stands out from the minimal-clean trend.

### The Output (Compound)

# Voss Type — Visual Branding Guide

## 1. Color Palette

### Primary Colors

**Soot Black — #1A1A1A**
Not pure black. Soot carries warmth and grit, like ink that sat too long on the press. This is the dominant color. It says: the work comes first, the polish is earned.

**Signal Red — #C8302B**
Confrontational without being juvenile. This is the red of a stamped warning, a boxing poster, a "FINAL NOTICE." It creates urgency and establishes that Voss Type does not whisper. Use it for emphasis, never as background fill.

**Raw Cream — #F2E8D5**
The paper stock, not white. White reads clinical. Cream reads like something was printed on something real. This is the resting surface for all layouts. It carries the hand-drawn work better than any neutral.

### Secondary Colors

**Bruise Purple — #4A2545**
For record label work and after-dark contexts. Deep, almost black at small sizes. Communicates a willingness to work in darker registers without losing sophistication.

**Hop Gold — #D4A030**
Warm and unapologetic. Connects directly to brewery and food brand work. Use sparingly: label accents, highlight strokes, social media punctuation. Never as a text color.

**Concrete Grey — #7A7A78**
Utility color. Captions, metadata, secondary information. Keeps the hierarchy clean when the primaries are doing heavy lifting.

### Psychological Rationale

This palette rejects the desaturated pastels and airy whites that dominate "modern branding studio" aesthetics. The combination of soot, cream, and red reads as industrial printmaking. It signals physical craft, decisive taste, and a willingness to be loud. The secondary colors expand range without diluting identity. A visitor should feel like they walked into a letterpress shop that also has a liquor license.

## 2. Typography Pairings

### Pairing One — Studio Identity and Website Headers

**Display: Hand-lettered custom logotype (see Logo section)**
Every major header on the Voss Type site should feel hand-touched. Commission or produce a set of lettered display phrases for recurring headers ("Work," "About," "Contact," "Services"). These are not fonts. They are drawn assets.

**Body: Founders Grotesk (or alternative: GT America)**
A grotesk with genuine character. Not geometric. Not neo-humanist. Founders Grotesk has the directness of a Swiss typeface but the proportions feel slightly compressed and assertive. Set body copy at 17px/28px for web. Left-aligned always. No justified text.

### Pairing Two — Case Studies and Client Presentations

**Display: Druk Wide Bold (Commercial Type)**
When hand-lettering is not appropriate (decks, PDFs, formal proposals), Druk Wide carries the same confrontational energy in a typeface. All caps, tracked tight. It fills the space the way your hand lettering does.

**Body: Untitled Sans (Klim Type Foundry)**
Quieter than Founders Grotesk. For contexts where the display type is already doing maximum work, Untitled Sans steps back and lets the content breathe.

### Pairing Three — Social Media

**Display: Your own hand-lettered Instagram type**
Every carousel, every reel title card, every story highlight cover should feature your hand. This is the single strongest differentiator. No studio using Helvetica Neue on a pastel background can compete with a hand-drawn title that looks like it was made with a housepainter's brush.

**Caption text: System font (no styling)**
Let Instagram and other platforms render captions in their default. Do not fight the platform for body text. Your visual identity lives in the image, not in caption formatting.

### Usage Rules

- Never set hand-lettered display type smaller than 32px equivalent. It loses its physicality.
- Body text is always a sans-serif grotesk. Serif body copy contradicts the studio's directness.
- All caps for navigation, labels, and metadata. Mixed case for body paragraphs and long-form.
- Minimum font weight for body: Medium (500). Light weights disappear against cream backgrounds.

## 3. Logo Style Direction

### Primary Mark: Hand-Lettered Wordmark

"VOSS TYPE" as a single lettered unit. Not a font. Drawn by hand, then digitized with minimal cleanup. Keep the stroke variation, the ink traps, the slight baseline wobble. The letters should feel like they were painted with a flat brush at speed, not a pointed pen.

**Characteristics:**
- Uppercase throughout
- Letters touch or overlap slightly. The wordmark is a single shape, not a row of separate characters.
- Stroke weight is heavy. This mark should hold at 40px wide on a phone screen.
- No tagline locked up with the logo. Taglines appear separately.

### Secondary Mark: "VT" Monogram

A condensed version for avatars, favicons, rubber stamps, and label corners. Two letters interlocked. The V and T share a vertical stroke. Keep it to two colors maximum (soot on cream, or reversed).

### Mark Variations

1. **Soot on Cream** — default, most uses
2. **Cream on Soot** — reversed, for dark backgrounds
3. **Signal Red on Cream** — accent use, social media profile photos
4. **Single-color Soot** — fax-machine-proof version for contracts and invoices

### What the Logo Is Not

- Not a geometric monogram in a circle
- Not a wordmark set in a purchased typeface
- Not symmetrical
- Not "clean." Clean is the enemy. The mark should look like a person made it, because a person did.

## 4. Imagery Guidelines

### Photography Style

**If you shoot your own process (and you should):**
- High contrast, slightly underexposed. Blown-out whites and airy lighting are banned.
- Shoot the mess. Ink-stained hands, torn sketches, brush water that has turned brown, reference printouts taped to the wall. The workspace is content.
- Crop tight. Fill the frame. A photo of a lettering sketch should not include the entire desk and a succulent.
- Acceptable color treatment: slightly desaturated with pushed blacks. Think documentary photography, not lifestyle blogging.

**Client work photography:**
- Always show the work in context. A beer label on the bottle, not floating on a gradient. A record sleeve held by a hand, not a flat mockup on marble.
- If using mockups, use only photographic mockups with real texture (wood grain, concrete, kraft paper). No floating-in-space renders.

### Illustration and Texture

- Scan real textures from your workspace. Ink splatter, paper grain, paint strokes. Build a library of 20-30 texture assets and rotate them across social and web.
- Hand-drawn borders, dividers, and ornamental elements are encouraged. But they must look drawn, not "hand-drawn font" drawn.
- Dingbats and spot illustrations (skulls, banners, arrows, ampersands) should be original. These become proprietary visual language over time.

### What to Avoid

- Flat vector illustrations in trendy outline style
- Gradient meshes or "aurora" backgrounds
- Any image that could appear on a Squarespace template preview
- Stock photography of any kind

## 5. Layout Principles

### Website

**Structure: Single-column dominant, asymmetric breaks**

The primary content column is generous (720px max-width for text, full-bleed for images). Resist the urge to build a grid-heavy portfolio layout. Your work is bold enough to command a single column.

**Key patterns:**

1. **Homepage:** Full-viewport lettered hero (your name, drawn large, possibly animated with a rough hand-drawn frame-by-frame feel). Scroll reveals three to five project thumbnails, each full-width, stacked vertically. No grid of squares. One image per project, big enough to read the lettering.

2. **Project pages:** Large image first, always above the fold. No "scroll past a paragraph of context to see the work." Body text sits below in a narrower column (600px). Pull quotes or key phrases rendered in your hand, inserted between body paragraphs as image blocks.

3. **About page:** Photo of you working (not posing). Text is direct and short. List clients by name, no logos grid. A single column of text with your lettered section headers breaking up the prose.

4. **Contact page:** Nearly empty. Email address, large. A hand-drawn "let's talk" or equivalent. Instagram handle. Nothing else.

**Spacing and Rhythm:**
- Vertical rhythm is generous. 80px minimum between sections on desktop.
- No card-based layouts. Cards imply containment and uniformity. Your work is neither.
- Horizontal rules are hand-drawn lines, scanned, not CSS borders.

**Navigation:**
- Sticky top bar, minimal. Logo left, four links right: Work, About, Services, Contact.
- No hamburger menu on desktop.
- Mobile: full-screen overlay menu with large hand-lettered page names.

### Social Media (Instagram Primary)

**Grid strategy:**
Do not aim for a "curated grid." That impulse is the clean-minimal trend wearing a different hat. Post the work. Let the grid be visually loud and varied.

**Post types (rotate these):**
1. **Final work showcase** — the finished label, cover, or wordmark. Full image, no border, no mockup template.
2. **Process shot** — sketch, ink study, brush test. Raw photo, minimal editing.
3. **Video/reel of hand-lettering in progress** — overhead camera, real time or slight speed-up. No music with lyrics. Ambient sound or instrumental only.
4. **Type specimen cards** — cream background (#F2E8D5), lettered word or phrase in soot black, your handle in concrete grey at bottom. These become shareable assets.
5. **Client testimonial or quote** — hand-lettered, not typeset. If a brewery owner says something good about working with you, letter it and post it.

**Carousel format:**
- Slide 1: The finished piece, large
- Slides 2-4: Process, details, context shots
- Slide 5: Brief text card with project name and your handle

**Stories:**
- Use the "hand-drawn highlight covers" mentioned above
- Stories are disposable. Use them for daily studio life, not polished content.
- Always use cream or soot backgrounds, never white or the default Instagram gradients

### Print Collateral

- Business cards: Letterpress on thick uncoated stock. Cream (#F2E8D5 equivalent in paper). Logo on front, contact info on back in Founders Grotesk. No QR code.
- Invoices and contracts: Simple. Logo top-left, body in Untitled Sans, concrete grey for labels, soot for values. PDF format. The documents should feel professional but not corporate.
- Packaging for self-promo mailers: Kraft paper, rubber-stamped VT monogram, hand-addressed. If you send a prospect package, it should feel like an object someone made, not something a fulfillment center shipped.

## Summary of Strategic Position

This system is built to do one thing: make it impossible to confuse Voss Type with a Figma-first, token-system, design-ops studio. Every touchpoint reinforces that a specific person with a specific hand made this work. The palette is warm and industrial. The typography is hand-drawn at every opportunity and assertively grotesque everywhere else. The photography is close, dark, and physical. The layouts give the work room to hit hard.

The trend right now is toward frictionless surfaces: white space, geometric sans-serifs, muted tones, rounded corners. Voss Type occupies the opposite position. Rough surfaces, drawn edges, saturated accents, tight crops. This is not contrarianism for its own sake. Your clients (food brands, breweries, record labels) need to stand out on shelves and in feeds. A branding studio that itself stands out on those terms is making a credibility argument: I can do for you what I have already done for myself.

## Part 2: The Decomposed Prompts and Outputs

Each prompt was run independently. No prompt had access to the other outputs. Each was given only the brief and told to address one dimension.

### Prompt 1: Color Palette

> I'm building a brand identity for Voss Type, a Brooklyn-based lettering and brand design studio. The founder's work is bold, hand-drawn, and confrontational. Clients are independent food brands, breweries, and record labels. Recommend a color palette with hex codes. For each color, explain what it does in the system (primary, accent, neutral, etc.) and why it fits this specific studio's positioning. Do not address typography, logo, imagery, or layout.

#### Output 1: Color Palette

**Primary: Soot Black `#1A1A1A`**

Role: dominant brand color, default text, backgrounds, lockups.

This is the workhorse. Hand-lettering studios live and die by contrast, and a near-black (not pure `#000000`, which vibrates on screen) gives the drawn letterforms a surface to push against. It reads as ink, as press impression, as the thing that hits the paper. For a studio whose work is confrontational, the primary color should feel like it already left a mark. Soot black does that without the clinical flatness of true black.

**Secondary: Raw Cream `#F2E8D5`**

Role: primary background, negative space, packaging surface simulation.

Warm, not white. This is kraft paper, butcher wrap, the uncoated stock that independent food brands and craft breweries actually print on. Pure white would signal corporate polish. Raw cream signals material honesty and positions the studio inside the physical world its clients operate in: shelves, tap handles, record sleeves. It also pairs with soot black at a contrast ratio that stays accessible without feeling sterile.

**Accent: Signal Red `#C8102E`**

Role: calls to action, highlights, selective emphasis, energy injection.

One accent color, used sparingly. Signal red is the color of rubber stamps, sale tags, wax seals, and the spot color on a two-color print job. It reads as urgency and hand-done authenticity simultaneously. For a studio serving breweries and record labels, red carries the right associations: live music posters, hot sauce labels, the one ink besides black that every letterpress shop keeps loaded. It should appear on no more than 10-15% of any given surface.

**Utility Neutral: Concrete `#8C8C8C`**

Role: secondary text, metadata, borders, UI scaffolding, captions.

A true mid-gray that stays invisible. It handles the information architecture (dates, nav labels, form fields, dividers) without competing with the black-cream-red system doing the emotional work. Concrete gray keeps the palette from becoming a three-color graphic identity that screams at every touchpoint. Some things need to recede.

**Tertiary (optional, situational): Overprint Gold `#B8860B`**

Role: limited edition markers, premium tier signaling, foil simulation on screen.

This is a conditional color, not a default. It exists for moments that need to say "this one is special" without reaching for a fourth saturated hue. Dark goldenrod reads as metallic ink on an uncoated sheet. It works when the studio needs to signal a collaboration, a limited run, or a portfolio feature tier. If the brand never needs that signal, this color stays in the drawer. That restraint is the point.

**System Logic:**
The palette is a two-color print job (black on cream) with one spot color (red) and a quiet utility tone (gray). That structure mirrors how the studio's clients actually produce packaging and printed goods. It means every brand touchpoint can be produced cheaply in two colors and still look intentional, with red and gold reserved for moments that earn the extra ink. The palette does not try to be playful, gradient-driven, or digitally native. It looks like it came off a press, which is exactly where a hand-lettering studio's credibility lives.

### Prompt 2: Typography Pairings

> Voss Type is a Brooklyn-based lettering and brand design studio. The work is bold, hand-drawn, and confrontational. Clients are independent food brands, breweries, and record labels. The color palette has already been defined (dark charcoal, signal red, raw cream, industrial gray). Recommend typography pairings for the studio's own brand materials. For each pairing, specify where it's used (headlines, body, captions, UI) and why it fits. Do not address color, logo, imagery, or layout.

#### Output 2: Typography Pairings

**Pairing 1: Druk Wide + Atlas Grotesk**

Druk Wide Bold (Commercial Type) for headlines, section titles, studio name in large-format applications. Druk Wide is compressed aggression. The extended width commands horizontal space the way a wheat-paste poster claims a wall. It reads as engineered loudness, not decorative loudness. For a studio whose output is hand-drawn, the brand typeface should not mimic the work. Druk is mechanical where the lettering is organic. That tension says: the hand makes the product, the system runs the shop.

Atlas Grotesk Regular/Medium (Commercial Type) for body copy, project descriptions, case study text, email communications. Atlas is a workhorse grotesque with just enough humanist warmth to stay readable at long lengths without turning clinical. It pairs with Druk because both come from the same foundry (optical consistency in metrics and spacing philosophy) but occupy completely different registers. Atlas disappears into the reading experience.

Atlas Grotesk Light (or Atlas Typewriter for monospaced contexts) for captions, metadata, image credits, date stamps, UI labels. Monospaced type in functional UI reads as workshop tooling, not design affectation.

**Pairing 2: Knockout + Galaxie Copernicus**

Knockout No. 48/No. 69 (Hoefler & Co.) for headlines, pull quotes, exhibition signage. Knockout was drawn from 19th-century wood type and fight posters. That genealogy is the point. The numbered width system (No. 48 for tighter settings, No. 69 for wider) gives the studio real control over how aggressive any given headline feels. A brewery label headline and a record label headline need different degrees of confrontation. Same family, different dial position.

Galaxie Copernicus Regular/Bold (Village) for body copy, long-form writing, case study narratives, printed collateral. A contemporary serif with visible stroke contrast and a vertical axis. It reads as literate and direct without the stuffiness of a Transitional face or the nostalgia of an Old Style. Copernicus Italic is genuinely calligraphic, not a sloped roman, making it a quiet nod to the hand-drawn core of the studio's work.

**Pairing 3: Obviously Wide + Pitch**

Obviously Wide Semi Bold (OH no Type Co.) for headlines, social media display text, merchandise, environmental graphics. An extended grotesque with optical distortion built into the drawing. Letters feel like they are under pressure, slightly warped by mass. That physical quality aligns with a studio whose lettering work has visible physical force.

Pitch Regular/Bold (Klim Type Foundry) for body copy, UI text, invoices, web interface, captions, metadata. A monospaced slab serif drawn for extended reading, not just code editors. The slab serifs give it a mechanical, stamped quality. Paired with Obviously's distorted grotesque energy, the combination reads as industrial workshop.

**Recommendation:** Start with Pairing 1 (Druk Wide + Atlas Grotesk). Most versatile across digital and print, easiest to implement in web environments, most forgiving at small sizes. Pairings 2 and 3 are stronger aesthetic statements but narrower in application range.

### Prompt 3: Logo Style Direction

> Voss Type is a Brooklyn-based lettering and brand design studio. The founder is a letterer whose work is bold, hand-drawn, and confrontational. The studio serves independent food brands, breweries, and record labels. Describe the logo style direction. Not a logo design. The principles, references, and constraints that should govern the mark. What it should feel like, what it should avoid, what makes it unmistakably this studio. Do not address color, typography, imagery, or layout.

#### Output 3: Logo Style Direction

**What It Should Feel Like:**

Pressure. The kind of weight you get from a wide-nib tool pressed hard into paper. The mark should feel like it was committed to in one motion, even if it wasn't. It should carry the physical memory of making: ink that pools at terminals, strokes that show their speed, forms that have thickness and stance.

It should feel owned. Not borrowed from a tradition, not referencing a style. It should feel like something this specific person would make and no one else would. The way a signature is illegible but unmistakable.

It should feel like it belongs on a wall, a window, a crate, a sticker slapped on a lamppost. Surfaces that are rougher than a screen. The mark needs to hold up in contexts where design goes to get beaten up.

**What It Should Avoid:**

Geometric construction. Nothing that looks like it was built on a grid, snapped to guides, or optimized for scalability before it was optimized for presence.

Smoothness. No Bezier curves that have been averaged into blandness. If the curves are clean, they should be clean because the hand was steady, not because the software corrected them.

Cleverness. No hidden symbols in negative space, no letterform tricks that require a second look to decode. The studio's work is direct. The mark should be direct.

Nostalgia. The studio works with breweries and record labels, which means every lazy reference point (Victorian lettering, tattoo flash, midcentury matchbook art) is already a cliche in those industries. The mark should not quote any of them.

Refinement that signals expense. This is a studio whose clients are independent. The mark should never feel like it costs $400 an hour to be in the room with it.

**What Makes It Unmistakable:**

The ratio of control to roughness. This is the signature of someone who letters professionally: the strokes are not sloppy, but they are not sterilized. There is a specific zone where craft meets aggression, where you can tell the person has drawn ten thousand letters and still pushes the tool like they have something to prove. The mark lives in that zone.

It should be reproducible by hand. If the founder couldn't redraw it on a napkin and have it still read as the same mark, it's too complicated.

It should be unambiguously a maker's mark. Not a corporate identity. Not a monogram from a stationery brand. Something closer to a cattle brand, a printer's device, a stamp pressed into wet concrete. A mark that says: this came from here, and here is a specific person with a specific hand.

**Reference Orientation:** Look at printer's marks from the 1500s. Not for style, but for function: a symbol compact enough to stamp on a title page, distinct enough to identify the shop, built from strokes that a craftsman could cut into wood. Look at how sign painters initial their work. Not the signs themselves. The small, fast, confident marks they leave in the corner. That is the energy.

**Constraints:** The mark must work at the size of a rubber stamp and the size of a building. The mark must be reducible to one color on any surface without losing its identity. The mark must not require context to read as Voss Type.

### Prompt 4: Imagery Guidelines

> Voss Type is a Brooklyn-based lettering and brand design studio with a bold, hand-drawn, confrontational aesthetic. Clients are independent food brands, breweries, and record labels. Define imagery guidelines for the studio's own brand presence (website, social, case studies). What kinds of images, what treatment, what to avoid. Photography style, texture use, how hand-drawn work is photographed or scanned. Do not address color, typography, logo, or layout.

#### Output 4: Imagery Guidelines

**Photography Style:**

Environment shots are shot in available light. The studio, the street, the bar, the print shop. No seamless backdrops. No infinity coves. If the background is messy, that is fine. A pile of reference books, a coffee ring on kraft paper, tape on the wall. These are not accidents to crop out. They are evidence.

Process shots are the priority. Hands holding pens. Brushes loaded with ink. Pencil roughs pinned to a wall. Cutting mats with blade marks. These images do more positioning work than any finished logo presentation. Capture them mid-stroke when possible. Blur from motion is acceptable and preferred over a staged, frozen hand.

Product and application shots (labels on bottles, menus in the wild, records on shelves) are shot in context, not in a studio. A bottle on a bar top with condensation. A menu tacked to a plywood board outside a restaurant. A record in a crate at a shop. The goal is proof of life, not a catalog image.

Portraits are direct. Straight gaze or mid-work concentration. No crossed arms. No leaning against brick walls with one foot up. No laughing-at-nothing. If the subject is working, shoot them working.

Lighting is natural or industrial. Overhead fluorescents, window light, desk lamps casting hard shadows. No diffused soft boxes. No ring lights. No backlit silhouettes.

**Hand-Drawn Work Capture:**

Scanning is the default for finished lettering. Scan at 600 DPI minimum, grayscale. Do not auto-correct levels. Preserve the full tonal range of the paper, including the tooth of the stock and any bleed from the ink.

Photographing lettering is appropriate when showing scale, texture, or the physical object matters more than legibility. A large brush piece on butcher paper, shot from a slight angle with visible paper curl.

Detail crops of letterforms are valuable. Get close enough to see ink pooling at terminals, brush hair texture in strokes, pencil construction lines under ink.

**Texture Use:**

Texture is embedded, not overlaid. Grain comes from the paper stock, the printing process, the photographic environment. It is not added in Photoshop after the fact. If a background needs texture, photograph an actual surface and use that photograph.

**Post-Production:**

Contrast runs high. Shadows go dark. Highlights stay warm, not blown. Saturation is pulled back. Not black and white, not sepia, not a named filter. Just less color than reality, with warmth kept in the midtones. Sharpening is minimal. No compositing. No mockups.

**Case Study Requirements (minimum):**

1. Process evidence: at least two images showing work in development.
2. The finished work on its actual substrate, photographed in the real world.
3. A detail shot isolating a moment of craft.
4. Scale reference: at least one image communicating how large or small the work is.

**Social Media:** 60/40 ratio favoring process over finished work. Stories can be rougher (phone camera, overhead desk angle, poor lighting). Still frames preferred over video.

**What to Avoid:** Stock photography. AI-generated imagery. Clean white backgrounds. Lifestyle staging. Matching flat-lay compositions. Highly retouched portraits. Grid-perfect Instagram feeds. Animated logos or motion graphics. Watermarks. Any image that could have been made by a studio that also does corporate annual reports.

### Prompt 5: Layout Principles

> Voss Type is a Brooklyn-based lettering and brand design studio. Bold, hand-drawn, confrontational aesthetic. Clients: indie food brands, breweries, record labels. The color palette, typography, logo direction, and imagery guidelines have already been defined. Define layout principles for the studio website and social media. Grid behavior, spacing philosophy, how density vs whitespace is handled, how the hand-crafted feeling survives in a digital layout. Do not address color, typography, logo, or imagery.

#### Output 5: Layout Principles

**Grid Behavior:**

The grid exists to be violated. Start with a 12-column grid at desktop, 4-column at mobile, then treat column edges as suggestions rather than rails. Elements should feel placed by hand, not snapped to guides.

Asymmetric anchoring. Every spread or section picks one element to anchor hard to the grid (a text block, a primary image) and lets the remaining elements drift. The anchored element tells the viewer "this is organized." The drifting elements tell the viewer "this was made by a person."

Column spanning is aggressive. Hero lettering pieces span 10 or 11 of 12 columns. Body text occupies 5 or 6 columns max, pushed to one side. The leftover space is not empty. It is pressure.

No centered layouts. Centering is the default of software, not the default of a hand. Everything pushes left, right, or diagonal.

Breakpoints are abrupt, not graceful. When the layout hits a breakpoint, it snaps to a new composition rather than reflowing smoothly. The mobile layout is a different poster, not a squeezed version of the desktop layout.

**Spacing Philosophy:**

Tight until it hurts, then tighter. Default spacing between elements is closer than comfortable. Lettering pieces crowd their containers. Text blocks press against images. Navigation items nearly touch. The overall sensation is density, compression, a poster stapled over another poster.

Breathing room is earned. Whitespace only appears when it is doing structural work: separating major sections, isolating a single portfolio piece for maximum impact, or creating a pause before a call to action.

Vertical rhythm is irregular. Uniform vertical spacing between sections reads as templated. Instead, vary section gaps deliberately. A tight 24px gap after a dense portfolio grid, then an 80px gap before a single hero image, then 32px before body text.

Margins are narrow. Page margins at desktop should be 3 to 4 percent of viewport width. Content pushes to the edges of the screen. The layout should feel like it is trying to escape the browser window.

**Preserving the Hand-Crafted Feeling:**

Rotational micro-offsets. Select elements rotate between negative 1 and positive 2 degrees. Not enough to read as a "design trick." Enough to break the pixel grid's perfection.

Imperfect alignment. When two elements share a visual edge, offset one by 4 to 12 pixels. Consistent enough to feel like a human placed it by eye rather than by coordinate.

Edge bleed. Let elements exit the viewport on purpose. A portfolio image that extends 15 percent past the right edge of the screen. A background texture that is cropped by the browser.

No hover transitions that feel like software. If hover states exist, they should be binary: on or off. No smooth fades, no 300ms ease-in-out.

Overlap as a compositional tool. Text over image, image over image, a navigation element that crosses into the content area. Build overlap into the layout grid itself, not as a decorative afterthought.

**Social Media Layout:**

Square and vertical formats are crops, not compositions. Start with the full layout, then crop aggressively.

Grid as portfolio wall. The Instagram grid functions as a single composition when viewed as a profile page. Alternate between dense multi-image collages and single bold lettering pieces. Plan posts in rows of three.

No templates. Every post layout is a one-off composition. The unifying element is the spatial philosophy rather than a repeating frame.

## Part 3: Comparison

### What the compound version produced

A coherent brand guide in one pass. Competent. Professional. A B+ deliverable. Every section was shaped by the same attention in a single generation. The color palette influenced the typography which influenced the layout. One voice, one perspective, one creative director making all the decisions simultaneously.

### What the decomposed versions produced

Five independent specialist reports, each significantly deeper than its compound counterpart:

- **Color** added a conditional fifth color with a rationale for restraint, and explained the palette as a two-color print job structure reflecting how the studio's clients actually produce packaging.
- **Typography** provided three complete pairing options with a recommendation and context-specific reasoning for when each works best, rather than two pairings.
- **Logo** went into the philosophy of the mark: "the ratio of control to roughness," reproducibility on a napkin, printer's marks from the 1500s as structural precedent. The compound version described a mark. The decomposed version described what the mark needs to feel like and why.
- **Imagery** specified scanning DPI, case study image minimums, a 60/40 process-to-finished ratio for social, and distinguished between scanning and photographing hand-drawn work.
- **Layout** introduced rotational micro-offsets, imperfect alignment as intentional design, edge bleed as a compositional tool, and the principle "tight until it hurts, then tighter."

### The structural difference

The compound prompt made the AI the creative director. One perspective made all the decisions. The output is coherent because one mind shaped it. That coherence is also its limitation: no tension, no surprise, no moment where one dimension pushes back against another.

The decomposed prompts made the AI five independent specialists. Each went deep on one dimension without being constrained by the others. Now the designer reads five reports and makes the decisions about how they fit together. The typography specialist might push in a direction that conflicts with the layout specialist. That tension is where the design happens.

**The compound prompt gives you the AI's vision of your brand. The decomposed prompts give you five specialists reporting to you. You are the creative director.**
